The Determinant of a Square Matrix (矩阵的行列式)

The determinant is defined only for square matrices. For a n×nn \times nn×n matrix AAA, the determinant of AAA, denoted as det(A)det(A)det(A), is a scalar value.

Rules of the Determinant

  1. If AAA is a square matrix and BBB is obtained from AAA by interchanging two rows, then det(B)=−det(A)det(B) = -det(A)det(B)=−det(A).
  2. If AAA is a square matrix and BBB is obtained from AAA by multiplying a row of AAA by a nonzero scalar ccc, then det(B)=c⋅det(A)det(B) = c \cdot det(A)det(B)=c⋅det(A).
  3. If AAA is a square matrix and BBB is obtained from AAA by adding a scalar multiple of one row of AAA to another row, then det(B)=det(A)det(B) = det(A)det(B)=det(A).
  4. det(kA)=kn⋅det(A)det(kA) = k^n \cdot det(A)det(kA)=kn⋅det(A), where kkk is a scalar.
  5. det(AB)=det(A)⋅det(B)det(AB) = det(A) \cdot det(B)det(AB)=det(A)⋅det(B), and det(Ak)=(det(A))kdet(A^k) = (det(A))^kdet(Ak)=(det(A))k for any positive integer kkk.
  6. det(AT)=det(A)det(A^T) = det(A)det(AT)=det(A)
  7. det(A−1)=1det(A)det(A^{-1}) = \frac{1}{det(A)}det(A−1)=det(A)1​ if AAA is invertible.
  8. det(A+B)det(A+B)det(A+B) is NOT equal to det(A)+det(B)det(A) + det(B)det(A)+det(B) in general.

Geometric Interpretation of the Determinant

Area and Volume Scaling Factor

The determinant of a square matrix can be interpreted as the scaling factor of the linear transformation represented by the matrix. Specifically, if AAA is an n×nn \times nn×n matrix, then the absolute value of det(A)det(A)det(A) represents how much the volume of a unit cube in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn is scaled when transformed by AAA. If det(A)=0det(A) = 0det(A)=0, it means that the transformation collapses the space into a lower dimension, and thus the volume is reduced to zero.

Computing the Area or Volume of a Parallelogram

For a 2×22 \times 22×2 matrix AAA, ∣det(A)∣|det(A)|∣det(A)∣ gives the area of the parallelogram formed by the column vectors of AAA.

For a 3×33 \times 33×3 matrix AAA, ∣det(A)∣|det(A)|∣det(A)∣ gives the volume of the parallelepiped formed by the column vectors of AAA.

The column vectors of a matrix can be thought of as the edges of a parallelogram (in 2D) or a parallelepiped (in 3D) that connects the origin to the adjacent vertices (相邻顶点). Note that the origin is also one of the vertices of the parallelogram or parallelepiped.

The Inverse of a Square Matrix (矩阵的逆)

Definition

For a n×nn\times nn×n square matrix AAA, if there exists a matrix BBB such that AB=BA=InAB = BA = I_nAB=BA=In​, then we call BBB the inverse of AAA, denoted as A−1A^{-1}A−1, and we say that AAA is invertible.

A matrix is invertible if and only if its determinant is nonzero.

A matrix that is NOT invertible is called a singular matrix, and a matrix that is invertible is called a nonsingular matrix.

Info

For a 2×22\times 22×2 matrix A=[abcd]A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}A=[ac​bd​], if det(A)=ad−bc≠0det(A) = ad - bc \neq 0det(A)=ad−bc=0, then AAA is invertible and

A−1=1ad−bc[d−b−ca]A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad - bc} \begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix} A−1=ad−bc1​[d−c​−ba​]

The inverse of a matrix has the following properties:

  1. If AAA is invertible, then A−1A^{-1}A−1 is also invertible and (A−1)−1=A(A^{-1})^{-1} = A(A−1)−1=A.
  2. For invertible matrices, (AB)−1=B−1A−1(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}(AB)−1=B−1A−1, (ABC)−1=C−1B−1A−1(ABC)^{-1} = C^{-1}B^{-1}A^{-1}(ABC)−1=C−1B−1A−1, etc.
  3. If AAA is invertible, then ATA^TAT is also invertible and (AT)−1=(A−1)T(A^T)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^T(AT)−1=(A−1)T.

Finding the Inverse of a Matrix

An n×nn \times nn×n matrix AAA is invertible if and only if AAA is row equivalent to the identity matrix InI_nIn​. And in this case, we can find A−1A^{-1}A−1 by performing the same row operations on InI_nIn​.
